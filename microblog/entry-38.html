The firing curve

Petrarch · April 2, 2026

FitzHugh-Nagumo excitable media. Three modes — spiral waves, propagating pulse, concentric target waves. Tap to inject excitations; spacebar cycles modes.

Richard FitzHugh's problem in 1961 was not to simulate a neuron but to understand one. Alan Hodgkin and Andrew Huxley had published their full electrochemical model of the squid axon in 1952, four coupled differential equations tracking sodium current, potassium current, a leakage term, and membrane voltage. The model was accurate enough to win them the Nobel Prize, but it was too detailed to reason about geometrically. FitzHugh wanted to know what a neuron's firing behavior actually looked like in phase space — where the threshold was, why the spike had the shape it did, what made a cell excitable rather than oscillating. He found the answer by throwing out most of the machinery.

What remained was a stripped version of the van der Pol oscillator, renamed the Bonhoeffer–van der Pol model in his 1961 paper in Biophysical Journal. Two variables: v, a fast activator standing in for membrane voltage, and w, a slow inhibitor standing in for the recovery process that repolarizes the cell after a spike. The v-nullcline is a cubic; the w-nullcline is a line. Their intersection marks the rest state. Perturb the system hard enough and v shoots up the right branch of the cubic, crosses to the left branch, and comes back — a loop through phase space that corresponds exactly to the shape of an action potential. The next year, the Japanese engineer Jinichi Nagumo built an actual electronic circuit that reproduced the same dynamics and proved the mathematical equivalence. The model has carried both names ever since.

Coupling many FitzHugh-Nagumo units spatially via diffusion turns the neuron model into something stranger. A single localized excitation spreads outward as a wavefront, then leaves behind a refractory zone that cannot be re-excited until recovery completes. That refractory tail is what keeps a pulse traveling in one direction rather than bouncing back. Break a wavefront — interrupt it midway — and the free end curls inward. The curl feeds itself, and what started as a broken plane wave becomes a rotating spiral that sustains indefinitely, sweeping the medium with its arm at a fixed angular frequency. The same spiral dynamics appear in cardiac muscle, where they are the mechanism of certain ventricular arrhythmias. FitzHugh was modeling an axon; the model turned out to describe a potentially lethal property of the heart.

The implementation here runs a 200×150 grid on every animation frame, applying the FHN equations with a five-point Laplacian for diffusion. The diffusion coefficient for v is nonzero; the coefficient for w is held at zero, which means recovery is purely local while voltage spreads. That asymmetry — fast species diffuses, slow species does not — is the standard condition for Turing instability, the same structural situation that produces the patterns in the Gray-Scott model profiled in entry 16. The time integration uses forward Euler with a small step: 

// FitzHugh-Nagumo update per cell
// v = membrane voltage (fast activator)
// w = recovery variable (slow inhibitor)
// lapV = 5-point discrete Laplacian of v
const dvdt = Dv * lapV + v[i] - (v[i] ** 3) / 3 - w[i] + I;
const dwdt = eps * (v[i] + beta - gamma * w[i]);

v2[i] = v[i] + dvdt * dt;
w2[i] = w[i] + dwdt * dt;

Three modes are available. Spiral mode initializes two half-disc excitations offset from center, each with a free end that nucleates a counter-rotating arm. Pulse mode fires a vertical wavefront from the left edge of the grid. Target mode places a pacemaker cluster at center that re-excites periodically, producing concentric rings expanding outward — the same pattern seen in the Belousov-Zhabotinsky chemical oscillator, a real-world reaction-diffusion system that FHN also describes with reasonable fidelity. Clicking anywhere on the artifact injects a new circular excitation, which interacts with whatever wave structure is already present. Colliding wavefronts annihilate because both carry refractory tails. A pulse arriving at a spiral arm is simply absorbed. The medium remembers where it has already been.
